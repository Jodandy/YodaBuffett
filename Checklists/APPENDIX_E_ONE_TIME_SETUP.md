APPENDIX A: PORTFOLIO MANAGEMENT RULES
═══════════════════════════════════════════

CASH MANAGEMENT
Philosophy:
"Cash is a position. Being patient is an action."
Guidelines:
Comfortable Cash Levels:

Normal market: 20-30% cash
Elevated valuations: 30-50% cash
Market uncertainty/volatility: 40-60% cash
Finding nothing attractive: 50%+ cash is fine

Never force investments:

Don't invest in "pretty good" opportunities just to be invested
Wait for genuine fat pitches (50-100%+ upside)
Missing opportunities is better than losing capital

Cash Management:

Keep in short-term vehicles earning 3-5%

Money market funds
Short-term Treasury bills
High-yield savings accounts


Maintain liquidity for quick deployment
No more than $250K per institution (FDIC limits)


POSITION MANAGEMENT
Portfolio Size:
Total positions: 8-15 companies
Why this range:

<8 positions: Too concentrated, single-company risk too high
8-12 positions: Sweet spot for active management
13-15 positions: Maximum before becoming index-like


15 positions: Too many to monitor properly



Quality over quantity:

Better to have 8 great companies than 15 good ones
Each position should be a genuine conviction
If you can't monitor it properly, don't own it


Position Size Guidelines:
Maximum Limits:

Single position: 15% (including appreciation)
Sector: 25% across all positions
Geography: 40% in any single country
Emerging markets: 20% total
Turnarounds: 10% total

Why these limits:

Prevents catastrophic portfolio damage from single failure
Forces diversification across themes
Allows concentration in best ideas
Protects against sector-specific crashes


Position Maintenance Rules:
Review positions <2% for exit:

STOP LOSS PHILOSOPHY
Stop losses are NOT about being "right" or "wrong."
They're about managing risk and protecting capital.
Key Principles:

The first loss is the best loss

Better to exit at -15% than hope for recovery to -80%
Ego is the enemy - admit mistakes quickly


Different strategies for different situations

Thesis-based stops for quality businesses
Technical stops for momentum plays
Hybrid approach for most situations


Stops are guidelines, not commandments

Use judgment
Consider market context
But don't make excuses for broken thesis




STOP LOSS STRATEGIES
Strategy 1: Thesis-Based Stops (Preferred)
Best for:

High conviction, quality businesses
Companies with strong moats
Long-term holds
Positions where fundamentals matter most

How it works:

Exit when original investment thesis is invalidated
Monitor key business metrics
Don't react to price volatility alone

Thesis Invalidation Triggers:
Exit if ANY of these occur:

Competitive Moat Breached

New competitor takes significant share
Product becomes commoditized
Pricing power disappears
Technology disrupted


Key Management Departure

CEO leaves unexpectedly
Multiple C-suite exits
Founder departure (if founder-led thesis)


Product Failure or Recall

Major product doesn't work as expected
Regulatory failure (FDA rejection)
Safety issues/recalls


Major Customer Loss

Loss of customer >20% of revenue
Customer concentration increases dramatically
Key partnership ends


Market Size Smaller Than Expected

TAM much smaller than thought
Adoption much slower than expected
Structural headwinds emerge


Financial Deterioration

DSO increases >30 days from entry
Cash flow turns negative 2+ quarters (while EBIT positive)
Debt/Equity ratio doubles
Customer concentration >10 percentage points higher



Example:
Original Thesis: "Software company has 60% market share in niche, 
switching costs are high, growing 25% annually."

Exit if:
- Market share drops below 50%
- Churn rate increases significantly
- New competitor gains >15% share
- Growth slows to <15% without explanation

Strategy 2: Technical Stops
Best for:

Lower conviction positions
More speculative plays
Momentum-driven stocks
When thesis is less certain

How it works:

Set price-based stops
Exit when technical support breaks
Protects against "thesis took longer than capital lasts"

Technical Stop Guidelines:
Large Caps (>$10B):

Stop loss: 20-25%
Reasoning: Less volatile, wider stops okay

Mid Caps ($1-10B):

Stop loss: 25-30%
Reasoning: More volatile, need breathing room

Small/Volatile Stocks (<$1B, biotech, etc.):

Stop loss: 30-40%
Reasoning: Very volatile, very wide stops

Recent IPOs / High Flyers:

Stop loss: 15-20%
Reasoning: Momentum can reverse quickly

How to implement:
Entry price: $100
Stop loss %: 25%
Stop loss price: $75

Set alert at $76 (gives time to evaluate)
If breaks $75, sell immediately (no debate)
Important:

Don't move stops lower (avoid "I'll just give it more room")
Do move stops higher as stock appreciates (protect gains)
Don't set actual stop-loss orders (can trigger unnecessarily)
Do set price alerts and monitor


Strategy 3: Hybrid Approach (Recommended)
Best for:

Most positions
Balances fundamental and technical
Allows flexibility with discipline

How it works:

Use technical stops as backup to thesis monitoring
Wider stops (25-35%)
But exit immediately if thesis breaks regardless of price

Example:
Entry: $100
Technical stop: $70 (-30%)
Thesis triggers: (list of specific fundamental issues)

Exit if:
- Price hits $70 (no matter what)
OR
- Any thesis trigger hits (even if price is $120)

Allows volatility but protects against:
1. Slow fundamental deterioration
2. Thesis taking too long to play out
3. Better opportunities emerging

Strategy 4: Time-Based Stops
Best for:

Catalyst-driven investments
Event-driven situations
When thesis has clear timeline

How it works:

Set expected timeframe for thesis to play out
Exit if catalyst doesn't materialize by deadline
Prevents "dead money" situations

Example:
Thesis: "FDA approval in Q2 2026 will unlock value"

Time-based stop:
- If FDA approval by June 30, 2026 → Hold
- If FDA approval delayed → Exit immediately
- If no approval by July 31, 2026 → Exit regardless

Prevents holding for years waiting for catalyst that never comes.

POSITION SIZING AS RISK MANAGEMENT
Stop losses aren't the only risk management tool:
The "Size Your Position for the Stop" Approach:
If you're willing to lose X% of portfolio on this idea:

Acceptable portfolio loss: 2%
Stop loss: 30%

Position size = 2% ÷ 30% = 6.7% maximum

Example:
$100,000 portfolio
Willing to lose: $2,000 (2%)
Stop loss: 30%

Position size: $6,700
If stop hits: Lose $2,010 (30% of $6,700)
Portfolio impact: 2%

This way, even if stop hits, damage is contained.

COMMON STOP LOSS MISTAKES
Mistake 1: "I'll Just Give It More Room"
Problem: Moving stops lower to avoid being stopped out
Solution: If you need to move stop lower, position size was too large
Rule: Never move stops lower. Only move higher (to lock in gains)
Mistake 2: "It's Only Down 15%, I'm Long-Term"
Problem: Confusing "long-term investing" with "ignoring deterioration"
Solution: Long-term ≠ Forever. Exit if thesis breaks.
Rule: Time horizon is about thesis, not about accepting losses
Mistake 3: "The Fundamentals Are Strong!"
Problem: Ignoring price action when technically broken
Solution: Price reflects information you don't have
Rule: Technical breakdown + fundamental questions = exit
Mistake 4: "I'll Wait for It to Come Back"
Problem: Anchoring to purchase price
Solution: Purchase price is irrelevant to future returns
Rule: Evaluate position as if buying fresh today
Mistake 5: "I Don't Want to Lock In a Loss"
Problem: Loss is already real (in portfolio value)
Solution: Selling doesn't "create" the loss, it recognizes reality
Rule: First loss is the best loss
