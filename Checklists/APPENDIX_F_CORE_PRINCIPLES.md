INVESTMENT PHILOSOPHY
The Fat Pitch Mindset:
"If the risk/reward isn't compelling enough to justify meaningful capital allocation, it's not worth doing."
This isn't about finding lots of opportunities.
It's about finding a FEW exceptional opportunities and sizing them appropriately.

THE 16 CORE PRINCIPLES
1. "Quality over quantity"
What it means:
Better to own 8 exceptional businesses than 15 good ones.
Why it matters:

Exceptional businesses compound wealth
Good businesses just keep up with market
You can't deeply understand 30 companies
Concentration builds wealth, diversification protects it

In practice:

Target 8-12 positions
Each position should be a genuine conviction
Don't buy just to be "invested"
One great idea > Five okay ideas


2. "Patience pays"
What it means:
Wait for genuine fat pitches. Don't force investments.
Why it matters:

Market gives you ~5-10 fat pitches per year
The other ~250 days, do nothing
Doing nothing is an active decision
Patience compounds through avoiding losses

In practice:

Comfortable holding 30-50% cash
Wait for 50-100%+ upside opportunities
Pass on "pretty good" ideas
Remember: "The big money is in the waiting"


3. "Size appropriately"
What it means:
Wins should move the needle, losses shouldn't devastate.
Why it matters:

Too small: 2% position up 100% = 2% portfolio gain (meaningless)
Too large: 20% position down 50% = 10% portfolio loss (devastating)
Position sizing is risk management

In practice:

High conviction: 8-12%
Medium conviction: 5-7%
Low conviction: 2-4%
Losses capped at 15% max
Adjust for volatility, culture, working capital


4. "Stay flexible"
What it means:
Adjust approach based on conditions and opportunities.
Why it matters:

Market conditions change
Your knowledge compounds
Rigid systems break
Flexibility isn't the same as lacking discipline

In practice:

Review checklist annually
Adapt to new fraud patterns
Change conviction levels as facts change
"When facts change, I change my mind"


5. "Think independently"
What it means:
Don't avoid stocks just because they've already moved.
Why it matters:

Mr. Market is bipolar (Buffett)
Stock up 100% can still be cheap
Stock down 80% can still be expensive
Focus on value, not price action

In practice:

Analyze from scratch every time
Ignore purchase price (sunk cost)
Don't anchor to 52-week high/low
Ask: "Would I buy this today?"


6. "Manage risk actively"
What it means:
Use combination of sizing, stops, and monitoring.
Why it matters:

One catastrophic loss wipes out years of gains
Risk management isn't about being "right"
It's about protecting capital
Offense AND defense matter

In practice:

Position sizing based on risk
Stop losses (thesis or technical)
Monthly monitoring
Exit triggers defined upfront


7. "Culture matters"
What it means:
Toxic management destroys businesses faster than competition.
Why it matters:

Culture shows up in Glassdoor before financials
Toxic culture = high turnover = execution risk
Great culture = retention = compounding
Management character > Management competence

In practice:

Check Glassdoor always
Reduce size if culture concerns
Exit if culture collapses
Green flag: Management buying stock


8. "People drive execution"
What it means:
Best strategies fail with wrong people.
Why it matters:

Strategy is easy, execution is hard
Management quality = business quality
You're betting on people as much as business
Cultural fit > Resume

In practice:

Check management background
Listen to earnings calls (tone matters)
Watch insider trading
Trust your gut on management


9. "Cash is truth, earnings are opinion"
What it means:
Always prioritize cash flow over accounting earnings.
Why it matters:

Accounting has flexibility
Cash flow doesn't lie
Frauds show up in cash flow first
Operating CF < EBIT = red flag

In practice:

Calculate OCF/EBIT always
If conversion <70%, investigate
If conversion <40%, likely fraud
Trust cash flow statement most


10. "If it looks too good to be true, it is"
What it means:
60%+ margins + extended payment terms = red flag.
Why it matters:

Exceptional margins usually mean something
Either amazing business OR accounting tricks
Extended terms suggest customers don't value product at stated price
Combination = vendor financing scheme

In practice:

High margins? Check why
Extended terms? Check industry standard
Both together

11. "Don't catch falling knives"What it means:
Let collapses stabilize before considering entry.Why it matters:

Stock down 50% can fall another 50%
"Cheap" doesn't mean "can't get cheaper"
Market often knows something you don't
Falling stocks have momentum downward
In practice:

If stock down >50%, wait 8-12 weeks
Look for stabilization (base formation)
Wait for insider buying
Wait for evidence of fix (not just promises)
Then maybe consider small position
Example:

Stock at $100 → falls to $50
Don't buy at $50 hoping for bounce
Wait for base at $45-50 for 2-3 months
Watch for insider buying
See sequential improvement in metrics
Then consider entry at $48
12. "Where there's smoke, there's fire"What it means:
One fraud indicator often means more are hiding.Why it matters:

Fraud isn't usually "one thing"
It's a pattern of behavior
First red flag you see is rarely the only one
Where management lies once, they lie twice
In practice:

Don't dismiss single red flags
Look deeper when you see one
Run full Fraud Detector if any concerns
Exit if pattern emerges
Don't give benefit of doubt
Example:

See "extended payment terms" mentioned
Don't think "well, just one thing"
Check DSO, check OCF/EBIT, check customer concentration
Often find 3-4 more red flags hiding
13. "The first loss is the best loss"What it means:
Exit at small loss better than hoping for recovery.Why it matters:

Down 20% needs 25% gain to recover
Down 50% needs 100% gain to recover
Down 80% needs 400% gain to recover
Psychology: Small losses easy to accept, big ones sting
In practice:

Use stop losses (thesis or technical)
Don't average down on broken thesis
Don't wait for "breakeven"
Accept small loss, move capital to better idea
Remember: Opportunity cost of dead money
Mental math:
Loss     Need to Recover
-10%  →  +11%
-20%  →  +25%
-30%  →  +43%
-50%  →  +100%
-70%  →  +233%
-90%  →  +900%14. "Hope is not a strategy"What it means:
Turnarounds require evidence, not management promises.Why it matters:

Management always sounds confident
Press releases are optimistic
But results matter
Evidence > Promises
In practice:

Don't buy on promises alone
Wait for evidence of improvement
Sequential data improving
Real operational changes
Insider buying supporting story
Red flags:

"We expect improvement in Q3"
"New initiatives will drive growth"
"We're confident about the future"
Without evidence, these are just words
Green flags:

Actual sequential improvement in metrics
Management making specific predictions and hitting them
Insider buying significant amounts
Customer testimonials/reviews improving
15. "Never fight the accounting"What it means:
If working capital deteriorating, believe it.Why it matters:

DSO doesn't lie
Cash flow doesn't lie
Management will explain it away
But numbers tell the truth
In practice:

Trust quantitative over qualitative
If DSO rising, it's a problem (regardless of explanation)
If OCF < EBIT, investigate (regardless of excuse)
If receivables growing faster than revenue, red flag (even if "explained")
Management will say:

"Timing differences"
"Large Q4 sales"
"Strategic customers"
"Industry standard"
You should think:

Is this true for competitors?
Is this new or historical pattern?
Is trend improving or worsening?
What does cash flow say?
16. "Management credibility is non-negotiable"What it means:
Any legal/fraud issues = automatic exit.Why it matters:

Trust is foundation of investing
Once broken, rarely restored
SEC investigations don't happen for nothing
Benefit of doubt = losing money
In practice:

SEC investigation = exit immediately
Auditor resignation = exit immediately
CFO sudden departure = investigate deeply
Class action fraud lawsuit = exit immediately
Insider trading charges = exit immediately
No exceptions. Ever.Even if stock drops 50% after news, still exit.
Better to exit at -50% than ride to -80%.PRINCIPLES IN ACTIONExample 1: Finding a Fat PitchWrong approach:

Research quality
Position sizing
Risk management
Discipline
Entry/exit decisions
Bucket 2: Things I Don't Control

Stock price day-to-day
Market sentiment
Macro events
Other investors' decisions
Focus 100% on Bucket 1. Accept Bucket 2.The "Regret Minimization" Framework:When making decisions, ask:
"What will I regret more in 12 months?"Example:
Stock with red flags but compelling valuation:

Regret A: Pass on it, it doubles (missed 100% gain on 5% position = 5% portfolio miss)
Regret B: Invest in it, it's fraud and drops 80% (lose 80% on 5% position = 4% portfolio loss)
Answer: Pass. Missing gains is less painful than losing capital.The "Circle of Competence" Model:Three zones:Zone 1: I understand this deeply
→ Can size 8-12%
→ Comfortable with volatility
→ Can add on weaknessZone 2: I understand this reasonably
→ Size 4-6%
→ More cautious
→ Don't average downZone 3: I don't really understand this
→ Don't invest (or max 1-2% speculation)
→ Admit what you don't knowMost investors lose money in Zone 3.
Most money is made in Zone 1.The "Margin of Safety" Model:Every investment needs multiple layers:
Valuation margin: Stock trading at 50% of fair value
Business quality margin: Moat protects against competition
Balance sheet margin: Low debt, high cash
Management margin: Trustworthy, competent, aligned
Size margin: Position sized for volatility
The more layers, the better.
If multiple layers missing, pass.DECISION FRAMEWORKSFramework 1: The "3 Questions" TestBefore ANY investment, ask:
"Can I lose 100% of this position without material harm?"

If NO → position too large



"Would I buy more if it dropped 20% tomorrow (assuming thesis intact)?"

If NO → conviction too low



"Can I explain this thesis to a smart friend in 2 minutes?"

If NO → don't understand it well enough


If all 3 are YES → Proceed
If any is NO → Pass or adjustFramework 2: The "Base Rate" CheckBefore assuming turnaround will work:Ask: "What % of similar turnarounds succeed?"Reality checks:

CEO turnarounds: ~20-30% succeed
Retail turnarounds: ~10% succeed
Manufacturing quality turnarounds: ~40% succeed
Working capital crisis turnarounds: ~5% succeed
Implication:
If base rate is 10%, your idea needs to be exceptional to beat the odds.Better: Focus on high-base-rate situations (quality businesses temporarily out of favor: 60-70% success rate)Framework 3: The "Premortem" ExerciseBefore investing, imagine it's 2 years later and investment failed.Write down:
"The investment in [Company] failed because..."Common reasons:

Management overpromised and underdelivered
Competition was fiercer than expected
Market size was overestimated
Working capital crisis emerged
Accounting was aggressive
Culture collapsed and talent left
Then ask: "How likely is each scenario?"If multiple failure scenarios seem likely (>25% each):
