# The Bank/REIT Pricing Paradox

## The Cycle

When interest rates went from ~0% to 4%+ in Sweden, two things happened simultaneously:

1. **REIT stocks crashed** — the market priced them as if high rates were an existential threat
2. **Bank stocks surged** — the market priced them as if high rates were pure profit

## The Contradiction

Banks and REITs sit on opposite sides of the same loan contract. The banks' "record profits" come from lending to the same property companies whose stocks got dumped. This creates a logical inconsistency:

- If REITs are truly in crisis, their loans are at risk of default — which means banks face massive credit losses and should **not** be at record valuations
- If banks are correctly priced (healthy loan books, no major credit risk), then the market implicitly believes REIT borrowers will service their debt — which means REITs should **not** be priced for distress

Both can't be winners and losers at the same time. They're codependent.

## What It Tells Us

The bank pricing quietly reveals the market's true belief: **Swedish real estate borrowers are fundamentally fine.** Handelsbanken, SEB, and Swedbank are not provisioning for a wave of property defaults. Their record earnings assume the loans keep performing.

The market just hasn't connected that conclusion back to REIT valuations. It's as if the bank analysts and the REIT analysts never talk to each other.

## Investment Implication

When a REIT like Brinova trades at a 50% discount to NAV — implying severe financial stress — but the banks lending to it are priced for smooth sailing, **the REIT price is more likely to be wrong.** The banking sector is effectively vouching for the debt, just in a language that REIT investors aren't listening to.

Some Counterarguments / Balance checks
A few worth considering:
1. Banks are diversified, REITs are concentrated
Banks don't just lend to property companies. Real estate might be 20-30% of their loan book. So even if RE loans go bad, it's painful but not fatal. The bank stock price reflects the whole business — mortgages, corporate lending, wealth management. So a bank can surge on high rates overall while still facing RE-specific risk in one segment.
2. Banks are senior in the capital structure
If a REIT defaults, the bank doesn't lose everything. They hold the properties as collateral, typically at 50-70% LTV. The equity holders (REIT shareholders) get wiped out first. So it's logically possible for banks to profit while REIT equity gets crushed — the banks recover their loans, shareholders don't.
3. Lag effect
Bank profits from high rates show up immediately in net interest margins. Credit losses show up later. So the market might not be "wrong" — it might just be early. The 2008 playbook: banks looked great... until they didn't.
4. Selective stress
Not all REITs suffer equally. Banks can be right that most borrowers are fine while specific REITs with high leverage still blow up. The average is healthy, but the tails aren't.
That said — none of these fully resolve the paradox. Especially point 2 is actually bullish for Brinova: if banks are confident in their collateral (Swedish residential), the underlying assets are sound, which means the NAV discount is excessive.
The paradox weakens somewhat but doesn't break. It still holds as a directional signal.
