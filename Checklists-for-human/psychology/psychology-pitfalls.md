# Investment Psychology: Pitfalls & Biases to Avoid

## Cognitive Biases That Sabotage Concentrated Investing

### 1. Anchoring Bias
**The Trap:** Fixating on the price where you first discovered a stock, making higher prices feel "expensive"
**Warning Signs:**
- Avoiding stocks because "they've already moved too much"
- Feeling like you "missed" opportunities after 20-30% moves
- Using your discovery price as the "fair value" reference point

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Judge each opportunity on current fundamentals, not price history
- Ask: "Would I buy this if I discovered it today at current price?"
- Remember: great companies often look expensive at every stage of their rise

### 2. Loss Aversion & Sunk Cost Fallacy
**The Trap:** Holding losing positions too long because you can't accept the loss
**Warning Signs:**
- Refusing to sell positions showing fundamental deterioration
- Averaging down on broken theses "to get even"
- Hoping dead money will "come back" instead of cutting losses

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Set clear exit criteria before entering positions
- Regularly reassess: "Would I buy this today knowing what I know now?"
- Remember: your cost basis is irrelevant to future returns

### 3. Overconfidence Bias
**The Trap:** Believing you can predict outcomes with more certainty than is realistic
**Warning Signs:**
- Sizing positions too large because you're "sure" about the outcome
- Ignoring risks or contrary evidence
- Feeling like you've "figured out" the market

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Always consider what could go wrong with your thesis
- Size positions to survive being wrong
- Seek out contrary opinions and evidence

### 4. Confirmation Bias
**The Trap:** Seeking information that confirms your existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence
**Warning Signs:**
- Only reading bullish analysis on positions you own
- Dismissing negative news as "short-term noise"
- Avoiding sources that challenge your thesis

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Actively seek bear cases for your holdings
- Subscribe to diverse information sources
- Assign someone to play devil's advocate on your positions

### 5. Recency Bias
**The Trap:** Overweighting recent events when making decisions
**Warning Signs:**
- Assuming recent trends will continue indefinitely
- Making strategy changes based on last quarter's performance
- Extrapolating short-term results to long-term expectations

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Look at longer time periods when analyzing trends
- Remember that markets are cyclical
- Don't abandon proven strategies due to short-term underperformance

### 6. FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)
**The Trap:** Making rushed investment decisions because others are profiting
**Warning Signs:**
- Buying stocks because they're "hot" or trending
- Entering positions without proper research due to urgency
- Increasing position sizes because you feel you need to "catch up"

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Stick to your research process regardless of market timing
- Remember: there will always be another opportunity
- Focus on your returns, not others' performance

### 7. Herding Mentality
**The Trap:** Following the crowd instead of independent analysis
**Warning Signs:**
- Buying popular stocks without independent conviction
- Selling when "everyone" is pessimistic
- Changing strategy to match current market favorites

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Develop independent investment criteria
- Be willing to be contrarian when analysis supports it
- Remember: the crowd is often wrong at extremes

## Concentrated Investing Specific Pitfalls

### 8. Position Sizing Errors
**The Trap:** Inconsistent or emotional position sizing decisions
**Warning Signs:**
- Sizing positions based on "gut feel" rather than systematic criteria
- Making largest bets on most speculative ideas
- Having too many tiny positions that don't matter

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Use systematic position sizing framework
- Adjust for volatility and liquidity consistently
- Regular portfolio reviews to ensure sizing discipline

### 9. Strategy Switching
**The Trap:** Abandoning your approach during difficult periods
**Warning Signs:**
- Constantly questioning your investment method
- Switching between value, growth, momentum strategies
- Making dramatic changes after short periods of underperformance

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Commit to approaches for 2-3 year periods minimum
- Track decision-making process, not just returns
- Remember: all strategies have periods of underperformance

### 10. Cash Deployment Pressure
**The Trap:** Feeling obligated to invest cash even when opportunities are scarce
**Warning Signs:**
- Lowering standards to deploy capital
- Buying "pretty good" opportunities instead of waiting for great ones
- Feeling guilty about holding cash during bull markets

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Remember: cash is optionality, not inefficiency
- Set clear criteria and stick to them
- Consider cash as paying you an option premium for flexibility

### 11. Analysis Paralysis vs. Insufficient Research
**The Trap:** Either over-researching to avoid decisions or under-researching due to excitement
**Warning Signs:**
- Endlessly researching without taking action
- Making investment decisions based on headlines or tips
- Having 50+ companies on "watch lists" but never buying

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Set research deadlines and decision criteria
- Distinguish between "nice to know" and "need to know" information
- Use checklists to ensure adequate but not excessive analysis

### 12. Narrative Fallacy
**The Trap:** Falling in love with compelling stories rather than focusing on fundamentals
**Warning Signs:**
- Ignoring valuation because the "story" is exciting
- Holding positions despite deteriorating fundamentals because you believe in the vision
- Buying stocks based on media narratives

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Separate compelling stories from investment merit
- Focus on quantifiable business metrics
- Ask: "What would have to happen for this story to be wrong?"

## Psychological Warning Signs

### 13. Emotional Decision Making
**Red Flags:**
- Making investment decisions when angry, fearful, or euphoric
- Checking portfolio values multiple times per day
- Losing sleep over investment performance
- Making sudden strategy changes based on recent results

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Implement "cooling off" periods before major decisions
- Set specific times for portfolio review (weekly/monthly)
- Develop systematic processes that reduce emotional input

### 14. Social Pressure & Comparison
**Red Flags:**
- Feeling pressure to match others' returns
- Sharing positions seeking validation
- Making decisions based on what "successful" investors are doing
- Feeling embarrassed about underperformance

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Focus on your process and long-term goals
- Avoid constant performance comparisons
- Remember: everyone's situation and goals are different

### 15. Overactivity & Trading Addiction
**Red Flags:**
- Constantly searching for new positions
- Unable to sit still with current holdings
- Making trades for entertainment value
- Feeling withdrawal when markets are closed

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Recognize that activity ≠ progress
- Set limits on research and trading frequency
- Find other outlets for intellectual stimulation

## Daily Practice Reminders

### Morning Questions:
- Am I making this decision based on logic or emotion?
- What biases might be influencing my thinking right now?
- Am I rushing this decision or taking appropriate time?

### Weekly Review Questions:
- Did I stick to my investment criteria this week?
- What decisions did I make and why?
- What biases did I notice affecting my judgment?

### Monthly Reflection:
- Am I following my stated investment philosophy?
- What patterns of bias am I seeing in my decisions?
- Do I need to adjust my process to address recurring issues?

## Emergency Bias Checks

**When you feel strong conviction about a decision:**
1. Sleep on it - emotions are often strongest immediately
2. Write down three reasons you could be wrong
3. Ask: "Am I seeing what I want to see?"
4. Consider position sizing: if you're wrong, what's the impact?

**When markets are volatile:**
1. Review your long-term plan before making changes
2. Ask: "Is this a time to be greedy or fearful?"
3. Check if you're making decisions based on recent news
4. Remember: volatility is the price of returns

**When performance is poor:**
1. Distinguish between bad process and bad results
2. Ask: "Am I abandoning my strategy at the worst time?"
3. Review whether fundamental thesis has changed
4. Consider if you're comparing appropriate time periods

Remember: The goal isn't to eliminate all biases (impossible) but to recognize them and have systems in place to minimize their impact on your investment decisions.
